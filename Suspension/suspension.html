The Suspension




Leaf spring and MacPherson Struts, which is a typical suspension setup on a RWD car.

You can use different ways to attach the wheels to the car so they can move up and down on their springs and dampers and do so with minimal change in the distance between adjacent wheels or in the near-vetical angle of the tyres to the road. The front wheels must be able to freely turn on their steering swivels. The driven wheels, whether the front, rear, or both, must be able to freely spin with the drive shafts as well.

Non-independent Suspension

A RWD (Rear-Wheel-Drive) car often uses a live axle, a tube containing both the driveshafts (half shafts) and the differential gears. A 4WD (Four-Wheel-Drive) may also have a live front axle as well. A dead axle (also called rigid beam), is now only used at the front on vans and trucks. Some FWD (Front-Wheel-Drive) cars have a dead axle. A rigid axle will have springs and links to prevent sideways movement.

Independent Suspension

In an independent suspension setup, each wheel on a car is independently attached to the body or the subframe. When driven wheels are independently suspended, the wheels are driven by jointed driveshafts that are connected to a differential that's fixed to the frame.

There's five types of suspension setups that are commonly used.

Double wishbones, which are commonly used at the front. There's two wishbones, one is above the other, to keep the wheel vertical as it go up and down.

MacPherson-strut suspension, which can be used at both the front or the rear. The wheel hub is fixed to a strut which has it's top end anchored to the frame or a reinforced fender. On the front wheels, the struts are able to rotate to allow steering. Pivoted arms extend inwards and forwards to the frame in order to keep the wheel vertical and to resist accelerating/decelerating forces.

A trailing suspension in a RWD drivetrain. The arm is attached to the rear wheel hub and widens into a V that pivots on the frame. The differential is fixed to the frame and the driveshaft uses universal joints.

A trailing arm is attached to the wheel hub on one end and extends forwards to a pivot on the frame. The arm may be broadened into a V shape with two pivots, either side-by-side or with the inner pivot slightly behind the front one. This setup is called a semi-trailing arm setup. Trailing arms are usually found only at the rear.

A Leading arm, which is only used at the front, is the opposite of a trailing arm. The wheel is in front of the pivot.

Swing axles are a system which is like a beam axle cut in half and attached to the pivots on the frame. They can be used at the front or the rear.

Usually the half-axle is broadened into a V with front and rear pivots to keep it from twisting.

Anti-Roll Bars

Pivots allows the bar to twist up to a limited angle so the car doesn't roll too much.

To prevent cars from rolling over while cornering, an anti-roll bar is used. They're usually used at the front, sometimes at the back, and sometimes both.

It's a torsion bar crossing the car through 2 pivots on opposite sides of the frame. Outside the pivots, the bar bends back and one end is attached to each wheel, usually through one or more flexible rubber bushings. When one wheel moves up, the other wheel gets pulled up by the bar as well, keeping the car level.
